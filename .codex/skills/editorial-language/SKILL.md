# Editorial Language

Write as the portfolio owner: precise, plainspoken, and grounded in the work.

## Rules

- Use short, concrete sentences. Prefer the actual product, decision, constraint, method, result, or limitation over a broad claim.
- Do not add generic AI language such as “seamlessly,” “cutting-edge,” “powerful,” “robust,” “leverage,” “revolutionize,” “unlock,” “delve,” or “not just X, but Y.”
- Do not make unverified claims, inflate outcomes, or hide limitations behind polished language.
- Do not use em dashes (`—`) or en dashes (`–`) in user-facing copy. Use a period, comma, colon, or parentheses instead.
- Keep normal hyphens only when they form a necessary compound, identifier, URL, command, code token, or numeric range.
- Preserve the author’s meaning, tone, and evidence. Do not rewrite text merely to make it sound more promotional.

## Editing workflow

1. Identify the supported facts and retain them.
2. Remove filler and replace abstractions with the concrete noun or action already present in the source.
3. Read the revised copy aloud. If it sounds like product marketing rather than an informed practitioner, simplify it.
4. Before finishing, scan every changed user-facing string for `—` and `–` and remove them.

## Examples

- Prefer: “The model compares staffing changes under matched simulated conditions.”
- Avoid: “A powerful AI system that unlocks seamless operational insight.”
- Prefer: “The result is directional evidence, not a production guarantee.”
- Avoid: “The result is clear — it transforms decision-making.”
